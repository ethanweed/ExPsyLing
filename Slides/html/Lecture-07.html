Word superiority paradox

  1. Words are composed of letters
  2. So, to read words, we first have to identify the letters
  3. But, we can read the letters faster if they are in a word
  4. Which must mean that we identify the word before we read the letters..?!?

Cognitive models of VWR

Search models: a mental card catalog

Forster’s bin model

  • bins of orthographically similar words
  • bins are sorted by frequency
  • each word has a pointer to a lexical item
  • lexical items have semantic connections

Activation models

Morten’s Logogen model

  • Different words have different activation thresholds (to account for the frequency effect)
  • Evidence acrues for different words
  • No real interaction

McClelland & Rumelhart’s Interactive activation model

  • upstream flow of information
  • hierarchies of representations
  • downstream flow of information
  • inhibitory connections between nodes at the same level

Coltheart’s Dual Route Cascaded Model

  • interactive activation on the “lexical route”
  • one-way, direct connection on the “non-lexical route”
  • can explain a lot of phenomena, but overly complex?

Paralell Distributed Processing models

  • e.g. Seidenberg and McClelland (1989)
  • there is no “lexicon”
  • it’s all just interactions, man

Bayesian Reader model

  • Readers assess the probability of stimuli corresponding to any given word
  • Readers start with a prior probability
  • As new information comes in, they re-assess
  • Makes no assumptions about underlying cognitive architecture
