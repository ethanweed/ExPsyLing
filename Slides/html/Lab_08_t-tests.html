Hypothesis-testing

  1. Not all statistically significant results are theoretically interesting results
  2. Not all theoretically interesting results are statistically significant

A little secret

Shh!! Don‚Äôt tell!

One-sample t-tests and paired t-test are actually the same thing.

One-sample: \[t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\hat{\sigma}/\sqrt{N} }\]

Paired: \[ t = \frac{\bar{D}}{\hat\sigma_D / \sqrt{N}} \]

Take-aways

Take-aways 1:

  • A one sample ùë°-test is used to compare a single sample mean against a hypothesised value for the population mean.

Take-aways 2:

  • An independent samples ùë°-test is used to compare the means of two groups, and tests the null hypothesis that they have the same mean. It comes in two forms: the Student test assumes that the groups have the same standard deviation, the Welch test does not.

Take-aways 3:

  • A paired samples ùë°-test is used when you have two scores from each person, and you want to test the null hypothesis that the two scores have the same mean. It is equivalent to taking the difference between the two scores for each person, and then running a one sample ùë°-test on the difference scores.

Take-aways 4:

  • You probably won‚Äôt ever use the \(z\)-test for anything

Take-aways 5:

All of these tests are basically the same:

\[ t = \frac{mean 1 - mean 2}{ variation} \]
